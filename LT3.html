LT3: Fractions and Rational Numbers

Fractions describe parts of a whole in a precise, comparable way. Rational numbers broaden this set by including negatives and repeating decimals. LT3 focuses on fluid operations with fractions: addition, multiplication, division, and simplification. We also convert mixed numbers to improper fractions and back to keep calculations consistent. Powers of fractions reveal how exponents transform ratios, including reciprocal behavior with negative exponents. Finally, we solve equations and compare fractions to sharpen decision making. By mastering these topics, you gain confidence in algebra and everyday quantitative reasoning.

Addition of Fractions

To add fractions with different denominators, first find a common denominator. Convert each fraction so they share the same base unit of measure. Add the numerators and keep the common denominator, then simplify if possible. For example, 1/3 + 1/6 becomes 2/6 + 1/6 = 3/6, which simplifies to 1/2. If denominators already match, such as 2/7 + 3/7, simply add numerators to get 5/7. Use the least common denominator to minimize conversions and arithmetic. Solid fraction addition supports clean totals in multi-step algebra and real-world measurements.

Multiplication of Fractions

Multiply numerators together and denominators together; no common denominator is needed. For instance, 2/3 × 3/5 = 6/15, which simplifies to 2/5. Always simplify the product to lowest terms for clarity. Multiplying by a fraction often scales a quantity down proportionally. This shows up in resizing recipes, adjusting models, and calculating combined probabilities. Keep factors visible to spot cancellations quickly. With practice, fraction multiplication becomes fast and dependable for rational expressions.

Division of Fractions

Division uses the reciprocal: a/b ÷ c/d becomes a/b × d/c. Flip the second fraction, then multiply and simplify. For example, 3/4 ÷ 2/5 turns into 3/4 × 5/2 = 15/8. Convert to a mixed number when helpful; 15/8 is 1 7/8. Division appears when splitting quantities or scaling down portions precisely. In algebra, reciprocal thinking supports solving rational equations cleanly. Comfort with this step prevents errors in longer, multi-operation problems.

Simplifying Fractions

Reduce fractions by dividing numerator and denominator by their greatest common factor. For example, 8/12 simplifies to 2/3 by dividing both by 4. Prime factorization helps reveal shared factors for larger numbers. Simplest form makes comparison faster and keeps work tidy. In algebra, reduced fractions are easier to combine and substitute. Always check for possible simplification after operations. This habit creates clean, readable answers and fewer mistakes.

Mixed Numbers and Improper Fractions

Mixed numbers combine a whole and a fractional part, like 2 1/3. Improper fractions show the same amount as a single ratio, such as 7/3. Convert a b/c to an improper fraction with (a×c + b)/c, so 2 1/3 becomes 7/3. Convert back by dividing and writing the remainder as the fraction. Using improper fractions makes operations consistent and fast. You’ll see these forms in measurements, timing, and everyday portions. Switching fluently reduces errors and keeps calculations aligned.

Powers of Fractions

Apply exponents to both numerator and denominator: (a/b)^n = a^n/b^n. For example, (2/3)^2 = 4/9 and (1/2)^3 = 1/8. Negative exponents invert first: (3/4)^-2 = (4/3)^2 = 16/9. Any nonzero number to the zero power equals 1, so (a/b)^0 = 1. Keep exponent rules consistent to avoid simplification errors. Powers help model growth, decay, and scaled transformations. They also prepare you for rational expressions in algebra and science.

Solving for Variables with Fractions

Clear denominators to simplify equations with fractions. If x/2 = 3, multiply both sides by 2 to get x = 6. Use the least common denominator when multiple fractional terms appear. Distribute carefully and track signs to avoid small mistakes. Check solutions by substituting back into the original equation. This builds reliable habits for rational problem solving. With practice, fraction equations feel straightforward and controlled.

Comparing Fractions

Convert to a common denominator so numerators compare directly. For 5/8 versus 3/4, rewrite 3/4 as 6/8 to see 5/8 < 6/8. Decimal conversion also works: 0.625 versus 0.75 is clear. Use benchmarks like 1/2 or 1 for quick intuition. Visuals can help when mental math gets messy. Comparisons matter for budgeting, fairness, and data interpretation. Choose the method that keeps your reasoning clean and fast.

Equations with Rational Numbers

Treat fractions consistently while preserving equality across each operation. Solve (x/3) + 2 = 5 by subtracting 2, then multiply by 3 to get x = 9. Use the least common denominator to clear multiple fractions quickly. Simplify before and after steps to reduce error risk. Rational equations appear in rates, averages, and proportions. They model real scenarios with shared units and varying amounts. Fluency here prepares you for multi-step algebra confidently.
